# Impact

## Definition

**Impact** asks:

**Did this work materially advance strategic goals or competitive position?**

Value and Impact are related, but they are not the same.

Value asks whether work created meaningful benefit.
Impact asks whether that benefit mattered in the larger strategic game.

A team can deliver something useful without it being strategically important.
A feature can help a few users while doing little to strengthen the company’s position.
A task can be well executed while still being the wrong place to spend scarce capacity.

Impact exists to make that distinction visible.

---

## Why it matters

Many organizations can justify almost any item in isolation.

A request sounds useful.
A stakeholder wants it.
A customer asked for it.
A competitor has something similar.
It seems easy enough to do.

But strategy is not about whether an item sounds reasonable on its own.
Strategy is about comparative choice under constraint.

That means Impact is about questions like:

- Does this strengthen a capability we need?
- Does this help us win where we are trying to win?
- Does this move us closer to a strategic objective?
- Does delaying this hurt our future position?
- Is this more important than the alternatives?

Without an explicit Impact lens, teams drift into:
- local optimization
- stakeholder-driven sequencing
- roadmap sprawl
- weak strategic coherence
- too many “important” items at once

Impact helps turn strategy from a slogan into a prioritization instrument.

---

## Impact vs Value

This distinction is important.

### Value
Did this create meaningful benefit?

### Impact
Did that benefit materially advance strategy?

For example:
- A feature might reduce support tickets for a small legacy workflow. That has value.
- But a different capability might improve adoption in the company’s target segment. That may have more impact.

Both can matter. But they are not equivalent.

---

## The core idea

A useful shorthand is:

**Impact is strategic consequence, not local usefulness.**

It asks whether the work changed something important at the level of goals, positioning, capability, or market advantage.

---

## What Impact includes

Impact often includes contributions to:

- strategic initiative progress
- capability-building for the future
- target market or segment traction
- competitive differentiation
- positioning strength
- product focus
- company-level goal movement
- higher-quality choices about what gets done and when

In VISTA, strong Impact often shows up when work does one or more of the following:

- strengthens a capability the organization needs to win
- improves odds of success in a chosen market
- clearly advances a stated strategic objective
- has a strong cost-of-delay case because delay harms future position

---

## Why Impact is especially important in the AI era

AI changes the economics of execution.

When work becomes easier to implement, organizations can suddenly pursue more of it. That sounds positive, but it creates a bigger need for strategic discipline.

If you can build more things, then:
- comparative prioritization matters more
- cost of delay matters more
- weak strategy gets exposed faster
- low-impact work can consume more capacity than before
- “we can do it” becomes a dangerous justification

Impact is what helps teams avoid mistaking expanded execution capacity for meaningful strategic progress.

---

## Typical signals of strong Impact

Indicators of strong Impact include:

- work clearly maps to strategic goals
- completed work strengthens an important product or business capability
- the change helps the company win in a target market or segment
- the priority decision looks correct in hindsight
- engineering capacity is increasingly directed toward high-cost-of-delay work
- lower-impact work is deferred or rejected with confidence
- delivered work contributes to a coherent strategic direction

Strong Impact usually requires both:
- a clear reason the work matters strategically
- evidence over time that the choice was correct

---

## Typical signals of weak Impact

Indicators of weak Impact include:

- many items can be justified as “important”
- teams cannot explain why one item should come before another
- work maps to vague goals but not to real tradeoffs
- strategy is used as decoration, not selection
- completed work helps locally but does not strengthen position
- priority decisions often look questionable in hindsight
- the roadmap is broad but not focused

One of the clearest signs of weak strategic clarity is:

**Teams cannot clearly explain why one item should come before another.**

That is often not a team execution failure. It is a strategy definition failure.

---

## Common anti-patterns

### 1. Strategy fog
The strategy sounds good in principle, but is too vague to order choices.

### 2. Objective mapping theater
Items are loosely linked to goals after the fact, but this does not improve real prioritization.

### 3. Stakeholder gravity
The loudest local priorities dominate sequencing.

### 4. Too many strategic priorities
When everything is strategic, nothing is.

### 5. Useful but low-impact accumulation
Teams do many individually reasonable things that do not add up to strong forward movement.

---

## Questions Impact should force

Before work starts:
- What strategic objective does this help advance?
- What capability does this strengthen?
- How does this improve our position?
- Why now instead of later?
- What better competing option exists?
- Why is this above that option?

After release:
- Did this choice hold up in hindsight?
- Did it help move a strategic initiative?
- Did it strengthen a capability we needed?
- Did it improve our position in a target market or segment?
- Was the cost of delay judgment correct?

---

## Suggested measures

Examples include:

- percentage of completed work clearly tied to strategic initiatives
- percentage of capacity spent on high-cost-of-delay work
- percentage of completed work that strengthens strategic capabilities
- percentage of work that improves target-segment traction
- hindsight quality of priority decisions
- rate of opportunity-cost mistakes discovered after the fact
- share of roadmap items with explicit comparative rationale
- proportion of delivered work later judged to have strategic significance

A strong Impact model is usually comparative, not purely item-by-item.

---

## ODIM example for Impact

- **Outcome:** Improve win rate in a target market segment
- **Decision:** Which product capabilities should be prioritized first?
- **Insight:** Which capabilities most influence buying confidence and onboarding success in that segment?
- **Measure:** Adoption and strategic traction of segment-specific capabilities, plus hindsight quality of the sequencing decisions

This is what makes Impact useful: it links measures back to strategic choices, not just release counts.

---

## What good looks like

A team with a strong Impact discipline:

- can explain priority order clearly
- rejects attractive but lower-impact work
- uses cost of delay to guide sequencing
- distinguishes local usefulness from strategic significance
- evaluates decisions in hindsight, not just at planning time
- treats strategy as a decision system, not a messaging layer

---

## Summary

Impact is the dimension that asks whether the work moved the company in the right direction.

It helps teams avoid confusing “useful” with “important,” and helps leaders see whether capacity is being spent on work that really matters.

**VISTA uses Impact to measure whether engineering is advancing strategy, strengthening position, and making better comparative choices over time.**
