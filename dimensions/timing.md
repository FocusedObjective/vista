# Timing

## Definition

**Timing** asks:

**Was scarce capacity directed to the right work at the right time?**

This is one of the most distinctive parts of VISTA.

Many frameworks measure what was delivered.
Some measure how fast it moved.
Very few ask whether the organization spent its effort on the work whose timing mattered most.

Timing exists to make that visible.

---

## Why it matters

A work item can be valuable and still be the wrong thing to do next.

Why?

Because engineering and product work are constrained by:
- attention
- sequencing
- coordination
- review capacity
- activation capacity
- organizational focus
- opportunity cost

This means the central prioritization problem is not just:
“Is this a good item?”

It is:
**“Is this better, right now, than the alternatives?”**

That is a Timing question.

Timing forces teams to think comparatively, not just individually.

---

## Timing and cost of delay

The most important concept inside Timing is **cost of delay**.

Cost of delay asks:
**What happens if we do this later instead of now?**

Delay may hurt by:
- slowing strategic progress
- weakening market position
- postponing customer benefit
- increasing competitive risk
- delaying learning
- missing a market window
- compounding operational pain
- preventing prerequisite capabilities from being built

In VISTA, Timing is not just calendar urgency.
It is about **the strategic consequence of delay**.

---

## The core idea

A useful shorthand is:

**Timing is about comparative choice under delay pressure.**

Or more simply:

**The best next item is not always the most obviously valuable item. It is often the one whose delay harms the organization most.**

---

## Why Timing matters more in the AI era

Historically, some sequencing decisions were constrained heavily by:
- team capacity
- specialist bottlenecks
- dependency structure
- implementation cost

Those still matter, but AI may reduce some traditional execution bottlenecks.

As that happens, the bottleneck shifts.

If execution capacity loosens, then comparative prioritization quality matters more.
The problem becomes less “can we build it?” and more:
- should we build it now?
- what better option exists?
- what gets displaced?
- what delay harms us most?

That is why Timing becomes a first-class metric dimension.

---

## Typical signals of strong Timing

Indicators of strong Timing include:

- capacity is increasingly directed to high-cost-of-delay work
- teams can explain why one item is above another
- opportunity-cost mistakes are identified earlier
- priority decisions hold up better in hindsight
- the organization rejects lower-timing-importance work with confidence
- strategic items do not languish behind locally urgent work
- sequencing reflects market, capability, and timing logic

Strong Timing does not mean all top items are rushed.
It means the sequence of work reflects the real consequences of delay.

---

## Typical signals of weak Timing

Indicators of weak Timing include:

- too many items are treated as urgent
- stakeholder pressure dominates sequencing
- lower-cost-of-delay work repeatedly gets done first
- teams cannot explain priority order clearly
- a better competing option often becomes obvious only later
- capacity is spread thin across too many initiatives
- timing is inferred from noise rather than disciplined comparison

One of the strongest signs of weak Timing is:
**priority decisions frequently fail in hindsight because a better competing option existed.**

---

## Common anti-patterns

### 1. Everything-is-urgent collapse
When too many items are urgent, cost of delay stops guiding real choices.

### 2. Loudest-stakeholder sequencing
Items rise because they have local pressure, not timing importance.

### 3. Ease-first prioritization
The easiest items win, even when delay on other work is more harmful.

### 4. Isolated scoring
Items are evaluated on their own, without serious comparison to alternatives.

### 5. Capacity diffusion
Too many moderate-priority items consume effort that should go to fewer higher-timing-importance items.

---

## Questions Timing should force

Before work starts:
- What happens if we delay this?
- What better competing options exist?
- Why should this be above those options?
- What gets displaced if we choose this?
- What strategic harm comes from waiting?
- Are we treating urgency as noise or as disciplined timing logic?

After delivery:
- Did this choice hold up in hindsight?
- Was the cost-of-delay judgment correct?
- What better option might we have done instead?
- Did this item deserve the timing priority it received?

---

## Suggested measures

Examples include:

- percentage of capacity spent on high-cost-of-delay work
- opportunity-cost mistakes identified before coding
- percentage of lower-cost-of-delay items chosen ahead of better alternatives
- age or delay of strategically important items
- hindsight quality of sequencing decisions
- number of initiatives in motion vs strategic focus
- proportion of completed work later judged to have displaced better options
- time spent on locally urgent but strategically weak work

The key is not perfect precision.
The key is building a real discipline of timing-aware comparative choice.

---

## ODIM example for Timing

- **Outcome:** Accelerate progress on the most strategically time-sensitive work
- **Decision:** Which item should be prioritized next?
- **Insight:** Which candidate item has the highest cost of delay relative to the alternatives?
- **Measure:** Capacity allocation to high-cost-of-delay work, plus hindsight quality of sequencing decisions

This is a good example of why timing cannot be judged from output metrics alone.

---

## What good looks like

A team with a strong Timing discipline:

- uses cost of delay as a real decision tool
- compares items against alternatives
- can explain priority order clearly
- recognizes that opportunity cost is part of delivery economics
- moves more capacity toward the work whose timing matters most
- treats sequencing as a strategic act, not just backlog management

Timing is not about speed for its own sake.
It is about making delay visible as a strategic variable.

---

## Summary

Timing is the dimension that asks whether the organization spent its effort where delay mattered most.

It helps teams move beyond isolated task scoring and into comparative prioritization, opportunity cost, and strategic sequencing.

**VISTA uses Timing to measure whether engineering effort is being directed to the right work at the right time, not just whether work is being completed.**
