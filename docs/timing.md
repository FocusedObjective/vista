# Timing

## Definition

**Timing** asks:

**Was scarce capacity directed to the right work at the right time?**

Many frameworks measure what was delivered.
Some measure how fast it moved.
Very few ask whether the organization spent its effort on the work whose timing mattered most.

## Why it matters

A work item can be valuable and still be the wrong thing to do next.

The central prioritization problem is not just:
“Is this a good item?”

It is:
**“Is this better, right now, than the alternatives?”**

## Timing and cost of delay

The most important concept inside Timing is **cost of delay**.

Cost of delay asks:
**What happens if we do this later instead of now?**

Delay may hurt by:
- slowing strategic progress
- weakening market position
- postponing customer benefit
- increasing competitive risk
- delaying learning
- missing a market window

**Timing is about comparative choice under delay pressure.**

## Typical signals of strong Timing

- capacity is increasingly directed to high-cost-of-delay work
- teams can explain why one item is above another
- opportunity-cost mistakes are identified earlier
- priority decisions hold up better in hindsight

## Typical signals of weak Timing

- too many items are treated as urgent
- stakeholder pressure dominates sequencing
- lower-cost-of-delay work repeatedly gets done first
- teams cannot explain priority order clearly

## Questions Timing should force

Before work starts:
- What happens if we delay this?
- What better competing options exist?
- Why should this be above those options?
- What gets displaced if we choose this?
- What strategic harm comes from waiting?

After delivery:
- Did this choice hold up in hindsight?
- Was the cost-of-delay judgment correct?
- What better option might we have done instead?

## ODIM example for Timing

- **Outcome:** Accelerate progress on the most strategically time-sensitive work
- **Decision:** Which item should be prioritized next?
- **Insight:** Which candidate item has the highest cost of delay relative to the alternatives?
- **Measure:** Capacity allocation to high-cost-of-delay work, plus hindsight quality of sequencing decisions

## Concrete example

A team has two items ready to start:

1. **Home page improvements** — moderate value, low risk, no deadline. Cost of delay: low. If it ships next month instead of this week, nothing changes strategically.
2. **Enterprise SSO provisioning** — complex, but three enterprise deals are waiting on it. Cost of delay: high. Every week of delay pushes at least one deal into the next quarter.

In a FIFO system or a "highest stakeholder volume" system, the home page improvements might win because they're easier or more visible. VISTA's Timing dimension asks: *what happens if each item waits?* The SSO work's delay creates measurable strategic harm. The home page's delay does not.

Interactive prioritization tools make this visible by letting leaders weight cost-of-delay higher and watching the ranking reorder in real time.
